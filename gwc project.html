Deforestation

What is deforestation?

Deforestation refers to the destruction or removal of trees by any means - intentional, accidental, or natural.

Causes

Some the main causes of deforestation are agriculture, and fires - natural forest fires, as well as those that occur as a result of slash and burn agriculture. As the population increases, so does the need for food, and forests are often converted into farm or ranch land. And while forest fires can be beneficial, they can also be detrimental if occurring in the wrong place, too often, or too severely. The amount of forest lost to fires is equal to that of logging and agriculture combined. Fires can change the structure of forests, leave them vulnerable to invasive species, threaten biodiversity, as well as alter water cycles and soil fertility.

Slash and burn agriculture is a method of cultivation in which forests are cut and burned. Farmers then plant crops in the soil fertilized by the ash. However, the land only remains fertile for a few years afterward, then it is abandoned.

Additional causes of deforestation include: commerical logging and clearing land for cattle ranches, as well as plantations of profitable trees like rubber and oil palm.

Impact

Due to habitat loss, deforestation can lead to the extinction of both plant and animal species. Especially as about 70% of land animals live in forests.

Trees maintain the balance of water in the environment, as well as prevent soil erosion. In deforested areas there is less water in the air that can be returned to the soil, leading to loss of soil arability. Without trees to prevent soil erosion, land can easily erode into rivers, landslides that damage homes and fields can occur as well. Some of the agricultural plants often used to replace trees can actually contribute to erosion. Since 1960, an estimated 1/3 of the world's arable land has been lost to soil erosion. As more arable land is lost, farmers continue to clear more forest to make room for their plants, continuing the cycle.

Furthermore, rainforest canopies also help regulate temperature, without them temperature variation is significantly increased which can be severely detrimental to species in the rainforest. Tropical forests alone contain more than 228 gigatons of carbon, which is more than 7 times the ammount emmitted by people annually. When these forests are cut, they release the carbon into the the atmosphere. Deforestation and forest degradation account for 15% of greenhouse gas emmissions. This contributes to aspects of climate change like global warming, weather and water pattern changes, and the increase in the number of extreme weather events. Changes in climate alter the environment of forests, making food and water less accessible. While some species may be able to adjust, others may not.

The burning of tropical forests can also heavily effect air quality.

Statistics

- About 2000 years ago, 80% of Western Europe was forested. As of 2019, it has become 34%.

- Around half the forests in eastern North America were cut in the time spanning the 1600s to the 1870s.

- Just over 20% of China is forested, having lost large amounts of forest over the past 4000 years.

- At least 772,204 square miles of land has been deforested and replaced with grazing pastures for cattle or sheep.

- Cropland accounts for about 18.9 million square miles of land, most of which was previously forested.

- About 1,931 square miles of the Amazon Rainforest is at least partially cleared annually. Partial logging leaves small patches of forest surrounded by cleared land, this can be extremely detrimental to wildlife.

- The United Nations Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO) estimates that the average rate of deforestation is currently 501,932 square miles per decade.
